Abstract Expressionism

Abstract Expressionism emerged as a distinct art movement in the United States in the aftermath of World War II and gained mainstream acceptance in the 1950s. It represented a major shift from the American social realism of the 1930s, which had been influenced by the Great Depression and Mexican muralists like Diego Rivera and David Alfaro Siqueiros. The term was first applied to American art in 1946 by the art critic Robert Coates, though Alfred Barr had used it in 1929 to describe works by Wassily Kandinsky. The movement was centered around the New York School, and it was the first specifically American movement to achieve international influence, putting New York City at the center of the Western art world, a role formerly held by Paris.

The roots of Abstract Expressionism lie in several earlier movements. It combined the emotional intensity of German Expressionism with the radical visual vocabularies of European avant-garde schools like Futurism, the Bauhaus, and Synthetic Cubism. It was also deeply influenced by the spontaneous and subconscious creation methods of Surrealist artists like Andre Masson and Max Ernst. An important predecessor was Surrealism, with its emphasis on spontaneous, automatic, or subconscious creation. Jackson Pollock's dripping paint onto a canvas laid on the floor has roots in the work of Andre Masson and Max Ernst. The arrival of European Surrealist emigres in New York during the war years, including Andre Breton, Marcel Duchamp, Max Ernst, and Roberto Matta, provided direct contact with avant-garde European ideas.

The political and cultural context of the post-war period was crucial to the movement's emergence. American social realism had been the mainstream during the 1930s. The political climate after World War II did not long tolerate the social protests of these painters. Abstract Expressionism arose during the war and began to be showcased at galleries in New York such as The Art of This Century Gallery, run by Peggy Guggenheim. The post-war McCarthy era was a time of artistic censorship in the United States, but if the subject matter were totally abstract then it would be seen as apolitical, and therefore safe. During the period leading up to and during World War II, modernist artists, writers, and poets, as well as important collectors and dealers, fled Europe and the onslaught of the Nazis for safe haven in the United States. Among the artists and collectors who arrived in New York during the war were Yves Tanguy, Max Ernst, Peggy Guggenheim, Leo Castelli, Marcel Duchamp, Andre Masson, Roberto Matta, Andre Breton, Marc Chagall, Fernand Leger, and Piet Mondrian. The post-war period left the capitals of Europe in upheaval, and New York replaced Paris as the new center of the art world.

Philosophical and Theoretical Foundations

Abstract Expressionism was deeply influenced by existentialist philosophy, particularly the ideas of Jean-Paul Sartre and Albert Camus about individual freedom, authenticity, and the anxiety of existence. The artists sought to create works that expressed profound emotional and psychological states, viewing the act of painting as an existential endeavor. Harold Rosenberg's concept of the canvas as an arena in which to act was pivotal in defining the approach of action painters. Rosenberg's critique shifted the emphasis from the art object to the creative struggle itself, with the finished painting being only the physical manifestation of the actual work of art, which was in the act or process of the painting's creation.

Clement Greenberg, the other major critic associated with the movement, took a different approach, emphasizing the formal properties of painting. Greenberg advocated for the flatness of the picture plane and the purity of the medium, arguing that each art form should focus on what was unique to its own nature. He supported Pollock's work on formalistic grounds as the culmination of an art tradition going back via Cubism and Cezanne to Monet, in which painting became ever purer and more concentrated in what was essential to it.

Carl Jung's theories of the collective unconscious and archetypal imagery also profoundly influenced many Abstract Expressionists. Artists like Pollock, Rothko, and Gottlieb drew on Jung's ideas about universal symbols and myths that reside in the unconscious mind, seeking to tap into a shared visual language that transcended individual experience. The influence of Native American sand painting, tribal art, and mythology was also significant, particularly for Pollock, who underwent Jungian psychoanalysis.

Key Characteristics and Visual Style

The movement comprised two main tendencies: Action Painting, characterized by energetic, gestural brushwork and physical engagement with the canvas, exemplified by Jackson Pollock and Willem de Kooning; and Color Field Painting, which involved large areas of flat color meant to evoke an emotional or spiritual response, exemplified by Mark Rothko, Barnett Newman, and Clyfford Still. Despite these differences, both approaches shared several key characteristics.

Abstract Expressionist paintings are typically large in scale, often monumental in size. This was a deliberate choice intended to overwhelm the viewer's field of vision and create an immersive, enveloping experience. The large scale also reflected the artists' ambition to create works of heroic significance that could stand alongside the masterpieces of the past. The all-over composition, in which the entire canvas surface is treated with equal importance and there is no single focal point, was another defining characteristic. This approach, pioneered by Pollock's drip paintings, eliminated the traditional hierarchy of compositional elements and created a visual field that seemed to extend beyond the edges of the canvas.

The emphasis on process and spontaneity was central to the movement's aesthetic. Whether through the gestural energy of Action Painting or the meditative layering of Color Field Painting, the process of creation was considered at least as important as the finished product. Many Abstract Expressionists embraced elements of chance and automatism in their working methods, allowing the unconscious mind to guide the creative process. The movement was also characterized by its rejection of traditional subject matter and representational imagery in favor of abstract or non-objective forms, though this was not absolute, as de Kooning's Woman series demonstrated.

Painting Techniques and Materials

Abstract Expressionist artists employed a wide variety of innovative techniques that broke with traditional painting methods. The movement's technical innovations were as revolutionary as its aesthetic ones, fundamentally changing what it meant to make a painting.

Drip painting, most famously practiced by Jackson Pollock, involved laying the canvas flat on the floor and pouring, dripping, flicking, and splattering liquid paint from above using sticks, hardened brushes, and even basting syringes. This technique eliminated the traditional upright easel and allowed the artist to approach the canvas from all four sides, creating an all-over composition without a clear top or bottom. Pollock described his process as being in the painting, walking around it and working from all sides. The physicality of his painting process, documented in Hans Namuth's photographs and films, became inseparable from the meaning of the work. Pollock used commercial house paints, particularly Duco enamel, which had a fluid consistency ideal for pouring and dripping.

The soak-stain technique was pioneered by Helen Frankenthaler in 1952 with her painting Mountains and Sea. She poured thinned oil paint directly onto unprimed canvas laid flat on the floor, allowing the pigment to soak into the raw fabric. This created luminous, watercolor-like effects on a monumental scale, with the color becoming literally embedded in the canvas rather than sitting on top of it. Morris Louis and Kenneth Noland saw Mountains and Sea and were inspired to develop their own stain painting techniques using the newly available acrylic Magna paints.

Impasto and palette knife technique was used extensively by artists like Willem de Kooning, who applied thick layers of oil paint with broad brushes and palette knives, then scraped and reworked the surface repeatedly. De Kooning often used newspaper to lift wet paint from the surface, creating textured impressions. His technique of continual revision, adding and subtracting paint over days and weeks, created richly layered surfaces that recorded the history of their own making.

Color Field painters developed techniques for applying large, even areas of color. Mark Rothko thinned his oil paint to a wash-like consistency and applied multiple transparent layers, creating his characteristic luminous, hovering rectangles. He left the edges of his color areas soft and feathered, allowing them to breathe and interact with adjacent colors. Barnett Newman used masking tape to create the sharp edges of his vertical zips, contrasting these precise lines with broad expanses of evenly applied color.

Franz Kline worked primarily in black and white, using house painting brushes to create bold, architectural compositions of sweeping black strokes on white grounds. Despite their apparent spontaneity, Kline carefully planned his compositions using small preliminary sketches that he projected and enlarged using a Bell-Opticon projector.

Major Artists and Their Contributions

Jackson Pollock became the most famous Abstract Expressionist, known for his drip painting technique. Laying canvases on the floor of his barn studio in Springs, Long Island, Pollock moved around and over the canvas, pouring and dripping paint from cans using sticks, hardened brushes, and even his hands. He controlled the flow of paint through the speed and angle of his movements, the height from which he poured, and the viscosity of the paint. The resulting webs of interlacing lines created complex, rhythmic compositions without a traditional focal point. His works such as Number 1A (1948), Lavender Mist (1950), Autumn Rhythm (1950), and Blue Poles (1952) exemplify the all-over composition where no single area dominates.

Willem de Kooning was known for his aggressive, gestural brushwork and his Women series, which controversially reintroduced figurative elements into abstract painting. His paintings combined violent brushstrokes with recognizable forms, creating a dynamic tension between abstraction and representation. De Kooning's technique involved constantly reworking his canvases, scraping away and reapplying paint in layers. His Woman I (1950-52) went through months of revision, with the art historian Meyer Schapiro encouraging the artist to persist when he was ready to abandon it.

Mark Rothko developed his signature style of large, luminous rectangles of color floating on a colored ground. His mature works were designed to envelop the viewer in an immersive emotional experience. Rothko insisted his paintings were not abstract but were about expressing basic human emotions like tragedy, ecstasy, and doom. He was extremely particular about how his paintings were displayed, insisting on dim lighting and close viewing distances. His Rothko Chapel murals in Houston (1964-67) represent the culmination of his vision, creating an environment of near-black canvases that envelope the viewer in meditative darkness.

Barnett Newman created paintings with large fields of solid color divided by thin vertical lines he called zips. His monumental work Vir Heroicus Sublimis (1950-51) measures over 17 feet wide and was designed to be viewed up close, overwhelming the viewer's peripheral vision. Newman's work explored themes of creation, the sublime, and the metaphysical. His painting Onement I (1948) marked a breakthrough in establishing the zip as his signature motif.

Helen Frankenthaler pioneered the soak-stain technique, pouring thinned paint directly onto unprimed canvas so the color soaked into the weave rather than sitting on top. This technique, first demonstrated in Mountains and Sea (1952), was enormously influential on Color Field painters like Morris Louis and Kenneth Noland. Frankenthaler's innovation bridged Abstract Expressionism and the next generation of color painters.

Other major figures included Franz Kline, known for his large-scale black-and-white paintings with bold, sweeping brushstrokes; Lee Krasner, whose work evolved through multiple stylistic phases and who was instrumental in promoting Pollock's career; Robert Motherwell, who combined intellectual rigor with emotional expression in his Elegy to the Spanish Republic series; Clyfford Still, whose jagged, flame-like forms of color created dramatic compositions; Adolph Gottlieb, known for his Pictographs and Burst series; and Joan Mitchell, who created vibrant, gestural abstractions inspired by landscape and memory.

Famous Works and Their Significance

Jackson Pollock's Number 1A (1948) is one of the defining works of Abstract Expressionism. The large canvas is covered with an intricate web of dripped, poured, and splattered paint in black, white, gray, and touches of color. The painting has no focal point and no orientation; it can be viewed from any direction. The layered skeins of paint create a sense of depth and movement that seems to pulsate with energy.

Willem de Kooning's Woman I (1950-52) was controversial upon its exhibition because it reintroduced the human figure into abstract painting at a time when many artists and critics believed that abstraction had transcended representation. The painting depicts a female figure with a ferocious grin, rendered in aggressive, slashing brushstrokes that merge the figure with the abstract background.

Mark Rothko's Orange and Yellow (1956) exemplifies his mature style: two soft-edged rectangles of warm color hovering on a painted ground. Despite its apparent simplicity, the painting achieves remarkable visual and emotional complexity through the subtle interactions between the color areas and the delicate modulations within each rectangle.

Barnett Newman's Vir Heroicus Sublimis (1950-51) is a monumental painting measuring approximately 8 by 18 feet, consisting of a vast field of red punctuated by five vertical zips of varying colors and widths. Newman intended viewers to stand close to the painting, allowing the red field to fill their entire visual field and create an overwhelming, sublime experience.

Relationship to Other Movements and Legacy

Abstract Expressionism was seen as rebellious and idiosyncratic, encompassing various artistic styles. It was the first specifically American movement to achieve international influence. The cultural reign of Abstract Expressionism had diminished by the early 1960s, while the subsequent rejection of the abstract expressionist emphasis on individualism led to the development of such movements as Pop Art and Minimalism. Throughout the second half of the 20th century, the influence of Abstract Expressionism can be seen in diverse movements in the U.S. and Europe, including Tachisme, Neo-Expressionism, and Lyrical Abstraction.

In Europe, Art brut and Lyrical Abstraction or Tachisme, the European equivalent to Abstract Expressionism, took hold of the newest generation. The radical anti-formalist movements of the 1960s and 1970s including Fluxus, Neo-Dada, Conceptual Art, and the feminist art movement can all be traced to the innovations of Abstract Expressionism. The movement fundamentally expanded the definitions and possibilities that artists had available for the creation of new works of art, and its innovations opened the floodgates to the diversity and scope of all the art that followed.

Contemporary art critics played a significant role in the development and promotion of Abstract Expressionism. Clement Greenberg advocated for Pollock and the Color Field painters, while Harold Rosenberg championed the action painters. Thomas B. Hess, the managing editor of ARTnews, promoted Willem de Kooning. The relationship between critics and artists was symbiotic, with critical writing helping to shape public understanding of the movement while the artists' innovations provided critics with material for their theoretical frameworks.
