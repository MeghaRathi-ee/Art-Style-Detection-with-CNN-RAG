Cubism Overview

Cubism was a revolutionary art movement that emerged in Paris in the early 20th century, fundamentally changing the course of Western art. Developed primarily by Pablo Picasso and Georges Braque between 1907 and 1914, Cubism rejected the single fixed viewpoint of traditional perspective and instead presented objects from multiple angles simultaneously, fragmenting them into geometric forms and reassembling them in abstract compositions.

Cubism is generally divided into two main phases: Analytical Cubism (1908-1912) and Synthetic Cubism (1912-1914). Analytical Cubism involved the systematic deconstruction of forms into faceted geometric planes, using a restricted palette of browns, grays, and ochres. The subject was analyzed from multiple viewpoints and these different views were combined in a single image, creating a complex, fragmented composition. Synthetic Cubism introduced brighter colors, simpler shapes, and the incorporation of collage elements such as newspaper clippings, wallpaper, and other materials pasted directly onto the canvas.

The origins of Cubism can be traced to several influences: the late works of Paul Cezanne, who sought to reduce natural forms to their geometric essentials; African and Iberian sculpture, which provided alternative approaches to representing the human figure; and the growing awareness that traditional perspective was merely one convention for representing three-dimensional space on a flat surface. Picasso's Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1907), with its fractured forms and African mask-like faces, is widely considered the first proto-Cubist painting.

Pablo Picasso and Georges Braque worked in such close collaboration during the development of Cubism that Braque later compared their relationship to being roped together like mountain climbers. Both artists moved through similar stylistic phases, producing works that were sometimes difficult to distinguish from each other. Their partnership introduced numerous innovations including collage (papier colle), the use of stenciled lettering, and the incorporation of sand, sawdust, and other materials into the paint surface.

Juan Gris joined the Cubist movement around 1911 and brought a more systematic, intellectual approach to the style. His paintings are characterized by greater clarity and precision than those of Picasso and Braque, with more vivid colors and more legible subject matter. Gris described his method as synthetic rather than analytical, starting with abstract geometric compositions and then introducing recognizable elements.

Other important Cubist artists include Fernand Leger, who developed a distinctive style featuring tubular, machine-like forms and bold primary colors; Robert Delaunay, whose Orphism or Simultanism emphasized brilliant color over monochrome analysis; Marcel Duchamp, whose Nude Descending a Staircase (1912) combined Cubist fragmentation with a sense of movement; and Albert Gleizes and Jean Metzinger, who wrote the first major theoretical text on Cubism.

Cubism's influence was enormous and far-reaching. It transformed not only painting but also sculpture, architecture, design, literature, and music. It provided the conceptual foundation for virtually all abstract art that followed, from Constructivism and De Stijl to Abstract Expressionism and beyond.


Cubism Key Artists and Major Works

Pablo Picasso and Georges Braque were the co-founders of Cubism, but the movement attracted many other significant artists who contributed their own distinctive interpretations.

Pablo Picasso's proto-Cubist masterpiece Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1907) depicts five nude women in a brothel, their bodies fractured into angular, geometric planes. The two figures on the right have faces inspired by African masks, while the others show the influence of Iberian sculpture. The painting shocked even Picasso's closest associates with its radical distortion of the human figure and its violent rejection of conventional beauty. It remained largely unseen until 1916 but is now recognized as one of the most important paintings of the 20th century.

Georges Braque's Houses at l'Estaque (1908) reduced the buildings and landscape of the southern French town to simplified geometric volumes, prompting critic Louis Vauxcelles to describe them as cubes, inadvertently naming the movement. Braque's contribution to Cubism was fundamental: he introduced the technique of passage (the opening of a plane's edge to allow adjacent surfaces to interpenetrate), the use of stenciled lettering, and the invention of papier colle (pasted paper collage).

Juan Gris (1887-1927) brought mathematical precision and vivid color to Cubism. His paintings, such as Portrait of Picasso (1912) and The Breakfast (1914), are characterized by clearly defined geometric forms, brilliant color, and a greater legibility than the works of Picasso and Braque from the same period. Gris worked deductively, beginning with abstract geometric structures and introducing representational elements, the reverse of Picasso and Braque's analytical approach.

Fernand Leger (1881-1955) developed a distinctive Cubist style featuring tubular, cylindrical forms and bold primary colors that reflected his fascination with modern machinery and urban life. His paintings, such as The City (1919) and Three Women (1921), combine Cubist fragmentation with a robustness and monumental simplicity that distinguish them from the more refined works of other Cubists. Leger's art celebrated the dynamism of industrial modernity with an optimism and vitality that anticipated Pop Art.

Robert Delaunay and his wife Sonia Delaunay developed a variation of Cubism known as Orphism or Simultanism, which emphasized brilliant, non-representational color arranged in circular and rhythmic patterns. Robert Delaunay's Simultaneous Windows series (1912) and Circular Forms series (1912-13) are among the earliest purely abstract paintings in the history of art.

Marcel Duchamp's Nude Descending a Staircase, No. 2 (1912) caused a sensation at the 1913 Armory Show in New York. The painting combines Cubist fragmentation with a representation of sequential motion influenced by chronophotography, showing a figure in multiple positions superimposed to suggest movement through time. Other significant Cubist artists include Albert Gleizes, Jean Metzinger, Francis Picabia, and the sculptor Alexander Archipenko.

The Revolution in Visual Representation

Cubism's significance extends far beyond painting. It represented a fundamental rethinking of the relationship between art and reality, challenging assumptions about visual representation that had been taken for granted since the Renaissance. The Renaissance system of linear perspective created the illusion that a painting was a window through which the viewer looked at a scene from a single, fixed point of view. Cubism demolished this convention, arguing that our actual experience of objects involves moving around them, seeing them from many angles over time, and combining these different views into a single mental image.

Picasso's Les Demoiselles d'Avignon of 1907 is widely considered the painting that launched the revolution. It depicts five nude women in a brothel, their bodies fractured into angular, geometric planes. The Introduction of Collage

A major development occurred in 1912 with the introduction of collage. Picasso created Still Life with Chair Caning by gluing a piece of oilcloth printed with a cane-chair pattern onto an oval canvas surrounded by a rope frame. Braque preceded Picasso in creating papiers colles, incorporating pieces of faux-bois wallpaper into charcoal drawings. These innovations fundamentally challenged the notion that art must be made entirely by the artist's hand and introduced the radical idea that real-world materials could be incorporated directly into the artwork. The use of newspaper, wallpaper, fabric, and other materials added new dimensions of meaning, texture, and visual complexity to Cubist compositions.

Sculpture and Architecture

Cubism's influence extended beyond painting into sculpture and architecture. Picasso's Guitar of 1912, a three-dimensional construction made from sheet metal and wire, pioneered the technique of constructed sculpture, building forms from separate elements rather than carving from a block or modeling in clay. This innovation influenced Constructivist sculptors like Vladimir Tatlin and Naum Gabo. In architecture, Cubist principles of geometric simplification and multiple viewpoints contributed to the development of modernist architecture, influencing architects such as Le Corbusier, whose early buildings reflect Cubist ideas about spatial analysis and geometric form.

The legacy of Cubism is immeasurable. It provided the conceptual foundation for virtually all abstract art that followed, from Constructivism and De Stijl to Abstract Expressionism and beyond. Its introduction of collage opened the door for assemblage, found object art, and mixed media installations. Its rethinking of visual representation influenced not only the visual arts but also literature, music, theater, and film.
