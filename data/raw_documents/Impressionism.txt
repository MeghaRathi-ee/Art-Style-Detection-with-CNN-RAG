Impressionism Overview

Impressionism was a 19th-century art movement that originated with a group of Paris-based artists whose independent exhibitions brought them to prominence during the 1870s and 1880s. The movement takes its name from Claude Monet's painting Impression, Sunrise (Impression, soleil levant, 1872), which was exhibited at the first independent exhibition in 1874 and prompted critic Louis Leroy to coin the term as a derisive label. The Impressionists rejected the rigid rules of academic painting and instead sought to capture the fleeting effects of light and atmosphere in the natural world.

The Impressionists made several revolutionary innovations in painting technique. They painted outdoors (en plein air) rather than in the studio, working directly from nature to capture the immediate visual impression of a scene. They used bright, pure colors applied in small, visible brushstrokes that blended optically when viewed from a distance rather than being mixed on the palette. They emphasized the changing qualities of light and its effects on color, often painting the same subject at different times of day or in different seasons. They rejected the smooth, polished finish of academic painting in favor of a spontaneous, sketch-like quality that conveyed the freshness of direct observation.

Claude Monet was the most dedicated practitioner of Impressionist principles. His series paintings, including Haystacks, Rouen Cathedral, and Water Lilies, systematically explored the effects of changing light and atmosphere on a single subject. Monet's late Water Lilies paintings, executed in his garden at Giverny, are vast, immersive works that border on abstraction and have been seen as precursors to Color Field painting and Abstract Expressionism.

Pierre-Auguste Renoir brought warmth and sensuality to Impressionism, particularly in his paintings of figures and social gatherings. Works like Dance at Le Moulin de la Galette (1876) and Luncheon of the Boating Party (1880-81) capture the play of dappled light on human figures in outdoor settings with extraordinary vivacity and charm. Renoir's brushwork is characteristically soft and feathery, creating a sense of warmth and intimacy.

Edgar Degas was associated with the Impressionist exhibitions but differed from his colleagues in important ways. He preferred to work in the studio from memory and sketches rather than en plein air, and his subjects were drawn from modern urban life, particularly ballet dancers, racehorses, and women bathing. Degas was a superb draftsman whose compositions often feature unusual angles, cropping, and asymmetrical arrangements influenced by Japanese prints and photography.

Camille Pissarro was the oldest member of the Impressionist group and the only one who exhibited in all eight Impressionist exhibitions. He was a dedicated plein air painter who explored both rural and urban subjects. Other important Impressionists include Alfred Sisley, who painted landscapes with delicate sensitivity to atmosphere; Berthe Morisot, who brought intimacy and psychological subtlety to domestic subjects; and Mary Cassatt, an American who became a central member of the French Impressionist circle, known for her paintings of mothers and children.

Impressionism's influence on subsequent art was profound. By liberating color from its purely descriptive function and emphasizing the artist's subjective perception over objective reality, the Impressionists opened the door for all the major modernist movements that followed, from Post-Impressionism and Fauvism to Expressionism and abstract art.


Impressionism Techniques and Famous Works

Impressionist painters revolutionized painting technique in ways that fundamentally changed the course of art history.

En plein air painting, working outdoors directly from nature, was central to Impressionist practice. The development of portable paint tubes (invented in 1841) and lightweight, collapsible easels made it practical for artists to paint landscapes and outdoor scenes on location rather than in the studio. This direct engagement with the subject allowed painters to capture the immediate, transient effects of natural light that were impossible to recreate from memory in the studio.

The Impressionist brushstroke was typically short, visible, and applied with rapid, spontaneous movements. Rather than blending colors smoothly on the canvas to create uniform surfaces, Impressionists placed distinct strokes of different colors side by side, allowing them to mix optically in the viewer's eye. This technique, sometimes called broken color, created a vibrant, shimmering surface that conveyed the sparkle and movement of natural light.

Claude Monet's series paintings represent the most systematic exploration of Impressionist principles. His Haystacks series (1890-91) depicts the same subject in different seasons, times of day, and weather conditions, demonstrating how radically the appearance of a scene changes with shifting light. His Rouen Cathedral series (1892-94) captures the cathedral facade in morning mist, blazing afternoon sunlight, and gray overcast conditions, transforming solid stone architecture into a screen for the play of light and atmosphere. His Water Lilies series, begun in 1895 and continuing until his death in 1926, eventually filled eight monumental canvases installed in the Orangerie in Paris, creating an immersive environment of reflected sky, water, and floating blossoms.

Pierre-Auguste Renoir's technique is characterized by soft, feathery brushstrokes and warm, luminous color. His Dance at Le Moulin de la Galette (1876) captures a crowded outdoor dance in Montmartre, with dappled sunlight filtering through trees and creating a kaleidoscope of colored shadows on the figures and ground. Renoir was particularly skilled at painting the effects of light on human skin, creating a sense of warmth and vitality.

Edgar Degas employed unconventional compositions influenced by Japanese prints and photography, with cropped figures, asymmetrical arrangements, and unusual viewpoints from above or below. His paintings and pastels of ballet dancers, such as The Dance Class (1874) and The Star (1878), capture figures in rehearsal and performance with a combination of acute observation and compositional daring. Degas also experimented extensively with pastel, developing a technique of layering and blending dry pastel with wet steam to create surfaces of extraordinary chromatic richness.

Berthe Morisot and Mary Cassatt brought distinctive perspectives to Impressionism. Morisot's loosely brushed paintings of domestic life and gardens possess a delicacy and intimacy that complement the more public subjects of her male colleagues. Cassatt's paintings of mothers and children combine tender observation with bold compositional choices influenced by Japanese prints.

Historical Origins and the Academic System

Impressionism emerged in a specific historical context that is essential to understanding its revolutionary significance. In mid-19th century France, the art world was dominated by the Academie des Beaux-Arts, which controlled artistic training, the annual Salon exhibition, and through these institutions, the careers of virtually all professional artists. The Academie championed a hierarchical system of genres, with history painting at the top, followed by portraiture, genre painting, landscape, and still life at the bottom. Paintings were expected to be carefully finished with smooth, invisible brushwork, muted colors, and clear linear compositions based on drawing.

The Salon de Paris was the primary venue for exhibiting art, and acceptance by the jury was essential for any artist's career. The jury, composed of academicians, routinely rejected works that departed from established conventions. In 1863, the number of rejections was so high that Emperor Napoleon III authorized the Salon des Refuses, allowing the public to see the rejected works. Among these was Edouard Manet's Le dejeuner sur l'herbe, which scandalized viewers with its depiction of a nude woman picnicking with fully clothed men in a contemporary setting.

In the early 1860s, four young painters, Claude Monet, Pierre-Auguste Renoir, Alfred Sisley, and Frederic Bazille, met while studying under the academic artist Charles Gleyre. They discovered a shared interest in painting landscape and contemporary life rather than historical or mythological scenes. Following a practice pioneered by the Barbizon school painters, they ventured into the countryside to paint in the open air. Their purpose was not to make sketches to be developed into finished works in the studio, but to complete their paintings outdoors, capturing the immediate visual impression of a scene.

By painting in sunlight directly from nature, and making bold use of vivid synthetic pigments that had become available since the beginning of the century, including cobalt blue, viridian, cadmium yellow, and synthetic ultramarine blue, they began to develop a lighter and brighter manner of painting. A favorite meeting place for the artists was the Cafe Guerbois on Avenue de Clichy in Paris, where discussions were often led by Edouard Manet. They were soon joined by Camille Pissarro, Paul Cezanne, and Armand Guillaumin.

The Impressionist Exhibitions

In December 1873, Monet, Renoir, Pissarro, Sisley, Cezanne, Berthe Morisot, Edgar Degas, and several other artists founded the Societe anonyme des artistes peintres, sculpteurs, graveurs to exhibit their artworks independently. Members were expected to forswear participation in the Salon. Their first exhibition was held in April 1874 at the studio of the photographer Nadar. In total, thirty artists participated.

The critical response was mixed. Monet and Cezanne received the harshest attacks. Critic Louis Leroy wrote a scathing review in Le Charivari in which, making wordplay with the title of Monet's Impression, Sunrise, he gave the artists the name by which they became known. The term Impressionist quickly gained favor with the public and was accepted by the artists themselves. They exhibited together eight times between 1874 and 1886, with shifting membership and increasing internal disagreements.

The artists of the core group gradually reduced over the years. Bazille had died in the Franco-Prussian War in 1870. Cezanne, followed later by Renoir, Sisley, and Monet, abstained from group exhibitions to submit their works to the Salon. Pissarro was the only artist to show at all eight Impressionist exhibitions. By the 1880s, the methods of Impressionist painting, in a diluted form, had become commonplace in Salon art.

Techniques in Detail

The Impressionists developed a systematic approach to representing the effects of light and atmosphere through color. Rather than using black or dark earth tones for shadows, as academic painters did, the Impressionists observed that shadows in nature contain reflected colors from the sky and surrounding objects. They painted shadows in blue, violet, and green, creating a sense of luminous atmosphere even in the darkest areas of their paintings.

New technology played a crucial role. Premixed paints in tin tubes, which became widely available in the 1840s, allowed artists to work spontaneously outdoors without the time-consuming process of grinding and mixing pigments. Many vivid synthetic pigments became commercially available during the 19th century, including cerulean blue, which became available to artists in the 1860s. The Impressionists' manner of painting made bold use of these pigments.

The Impressionists' progress toward a brighter style was gradual. During the 1860s, Monet and Renoir sometimes painted on canvases prepared with traditional red-brown or grey ground. By the 1870s, they usually chose grounds of lighter grey or beige. By the 1880s, some had come to prefer white or slightly off-white grounds.

Photography also influenced the Impressionists. As cameras became more portable, photographs became more candid, and photography inspired the Impressionists to represent momentary action. The development of Impressionism can be considered partly as a reaction to the challenge presented by photography, which seemed to devalue the artist's skill in reproducing reality. Rather than competing with photography, artists focused on the one thing they could do better: subjective expression of perception.

Japanese ukiyo-e art prints also had a major influence. The asymmetrical compositions, bold outlines, flat areas of color, and unusual viewpoints found in Japanese prints inspired Impressionist painters to experiment with similar compositional strategies. Monet, Degas, and Cassatt were particularly influenced by Japanese prints.

Major Artists in Depth

Claude Monet dedicated his entire career to the pursuit of Impressionist principles with a consistency unmatched by any of his colleagues. His series paintings, begun in the 1890s, systematically explored the effects of changing light on a single subject. The Haystacks series of 1890 to 1891 includes over thirty paintings of grain stacks at different times of day and seasons. His Rouen Cathedral series of 1892 to 1894 captures the cathedral facade under varying conditions of light, transforming solid stone into a shimmering screen of color. His monumental Water Lilies, which occupied him from the 1890s until his death in 1926, culminated in the huge panels installed in the Orangerie museum in Paris, creating an immersive environment that has been compared to an environmental artwork or installation.

Edgar Degas brought a unique perspective to Impressionism. Unlike his colleagues, he preferred working in the studio from memory and sketches, and he was primarily interested in figure subjects drawn from modern urban life. His paintings and pastels of ballet dancers, observed during rehearsals and performances at the Paris Opera, capture the physical effort and discipline behind the illusion of graceful movement. Degas was a superb draftsman who drew constantly, filling notebooks with studies of movement and gesture.

Mary Cassatt was an American artist who became a central member of the Impressionist circle after being invited by Degas to participate in the fourth Impressionist exhibition in 1879. Her paintings of mothers and children combine tender observation with bold compositional choices influenced by Japanese prints. Cassatt was instrumental in introducing Impressionist painting to American collectors, advising wealthy friends and acquaintances on their purchases.

Legacy and Influence

Impressionism's influence on subsequent art was profound and far-reaching. By liberating color from its purely descriptive function and emphasizing the artist's subjective perception over objective reality, the Impressionists opened the door for virtually all the major modernist movements that followed. Post-Impressionism, Fauvism, Expressionism, and abstract art all built upon foundations laid by the Impressionists. The movement also fundamentally changed the institutional structure of the art world, demonstrating that artists could exhibit and sell their work independently of official academic channels.
