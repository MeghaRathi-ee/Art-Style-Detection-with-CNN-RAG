Contemporary Realism Overview

Contemporary Realism is a broad art movement encompassing various styles of painting that prioritize accurate, realistic depiction of subjects while incorporating contemporary sensibilities, techniques, and subject matter. Unlike the strict academic realism of the 19th century, Contemporary Realism embraces a wide range of approaches to representation, from meticulous photorealistic rendering to more painterly styles that nonetheless maintain a commitment to depicting recognizable subjects.

The movement emerged in the mid-to-late 20th century as a reaction against the dominance of abstract and conceptual art in the mainstream art world. Contemporary Realist artists argued that representational painting remained a vital and relevant form of artistic expression, capable of addressing contemporary themes and emotions through the careful observation and rendering of the visible world.

Photorealism, also known as Superrealism or Hyperrealism, is a closely related movement that emerged in the late 1960s and early 1970s. Photorealist painters create works that are virtually indistinguishable from photographs, typically working from photographic sources and employing techniques such as projection and grid systems to achieve extreme precision. Key Photorealist artists include Chuck Close, who created monumental portrait paintings based on photographs; Richard Estes, known for his precise depictions of urban storefronts and reflective surfaces; and Audrey Flack, who painted still lifes with extraordinary detail.

Hyperrealism goes beyond Photorealism by creating images that are even more detailed and precise than photographs, often manipulating focus, color, and lighting to create an enhanced or super-real effect. Artists like Denis Peterson, Gottfried Helnwein, and Ron Mueck (in sculpture) have pushed the boundaries of realistic representation to create works that are both technically stunning and emotionally provocative.

Contemporary Realism encompasses diverse subjects including urban landscapes, portraits, still lifes, figure studies, and scenes of everyday life. The movement values technical skill, direct observation, and the ability to capture the subtleties of light, color, atmosphere, and human expression. Many Contemporary Realists employ traditional techniques such as oil painting, working from life rather than photographs, and following the practices of classical atelier training.

The movement has been supported by organizations such as the Art Renewal Center and galleries specializing in representational art. Contemporary Realism continues to evolve, with artists incorporating digital tools, diverse cultural perspectives, and contemporary social themes into their realistic paintings.


Contemporary Realism and Photorealism Techniques

Contemporary Realist painters employ a range of techniques from traditional academic methods to modern photographic reference methods, all in service of creating convincing representations of visible reality.

Photorealist painters typically begin by taking photographs of their subjects, which they then transfer to canvas using various methods. The grid method involves dividing both the reference photograph and the canvas into identical grids, then reproducing the contents of each grid square on the enlarged canvas. Some artists use overhead or opaque projectors to project the photographic image directly onto the canvas as a guide. The painting is then built up through careful application of paint, often using airbrush techniques for smooth, photographic surfaces.

Chuck Close revolutionized portrait painting by creating monumental faces from photographs, initially using an airbrush and later developing a distinctive grid technique in which each square of the grid contains a small, abstract painting that reads as a coherent portrait when viewed from a distance. His Self-Portrait (1968), measuring over nine feet tall, was among the first Photorealist portraits and demonstrated that photographic imagery could be translated into painting at monumental scale with astonishing precision.

Richard Estes specialized in urban scenes, particularly storefronts and street scenes with complex reflective surfaces. His paintings of glass, chrome, and polished metal achieve a level of detail and clarity that actually exceeds what the human eye can perceive, since he combines sharp focus across the entire image rather than mimicking the selective focus of human vision. His technique involved working from multiple photographs of the same scene, synthesizing information to create an idealized, hyper-clear image.

Atelier training methods have experienced a revival through the Contemporary Realism movement. Many contemporary realist painters train in ateliers that follow 19th-century academic traditions, learning to draw from plaster casts and live models, mix colors according to the Munsell system, and build paintings in systematic stages from grisaille underpainting through glazing and scumbling to achieve luminous, old-master-like surfaces.

The distinction between Photorealism and broader Contemporary Realism lies primarily in the artist's relationship to photography. Photorealists explicitly work from and reference photographs, while many Contemporary Realists work from direct observation or combine photographic reference with plein air studies and studio work from live models. Both share a commitment to technical skill and accurate representation, but Contemporary Realism encompasses a wider range of approaches and often allows for more visible painterly qualities such as visible brushwork and subjective color interpretation.

The Revival of Representational Painting

Contemporary Realism emerged in the mid-to-late 20th century during a period when abstract and conceptual art dominated the mainstream art world. Representational painters found themselves marginalized by critics and institutions that viewed realism as retrograde and irrelevant. The movement represented a deliberate counter-argument to this orthodoxy, asserting that the careful observation and rendering of visible reality remained a vital and meaningful form of artistic expression.

The atelier movement played a crucial role in the revival of traditional representational painting skills. Ateliers following 19th-century academic training methods experienced a renaissance beginning in the 1970s and 1980s. These schools, including the Art Students League of New York, the Florence Academy of Art, and numerous smaller ateliers, trained students in the systematic methods of drawing and painting that had been largely abandoned by art schools focused on modern and contemporary approaches. Students learned to draw from plaster casts and live models, to mix colors according to systematic methods, and to build paintings in stages from underpainting through glazing and scumbling.

Photorealism and Hyperrealism

Photorealism, which emerged in the late 1960s and early 1970s, represented one of the most technically demanding approaches within Contemporary Realism. Photorealist painters created works that were virtually indistinguishable from photographs, working from photographic sources and employing techniques such as projection and grid systems to achieve extreme precision.

Chuck Close revolutionized portrait painting by creating monumental faces from photographs. His early Self-Portrait from 1968, measuring over nine feet tall, was among the first Photorealist portraits. Close initially used an airbrush technique to achieve smooth, photographic surfaces, but after a spinal blood clot in 1988 left him partially paralyzed, he developed a distinctive grid technique in which each square contains a small, abstract painting that reads as a coherent portrait when viewed from a distance. This late technique created a fascinating tension between abstraction at close range and realism at a distance.

Richard Estes specialized in urban scenes, particularly storefronts and street scenes with complex reflective surfaces in glass, chrome, and polished metal. His paintings achieve a level of detail and clarity that actually exceeds what the human eye can perceive, since he combines sharp focus across the entire image. His technique involved working from multiple photographs of the same scene, synthesizing information to create an idealized, hyper-clear image. Audrey Flack created still-life paintings of extraordinary detail, often incorporating symbolic objects like skulls, candles, and mirrors that linked her work to the vanitas tradition of 17th-century Dutch painting.

Both share a commitment to technical skill and accurate representation.

Cultural Significance and Debates

Contemporary Realism has sparked ongoing debates within the art world about the relationship between skill and concept, tradition and innovation, observation and expression. Critics of the movement argue that technical skill alone does not constitute meaningful art, while supporters counter that the discipline of careful observation develops a depth of visual understanding that enriches artistic expression. The movement has been supported by organizations such as the Art Renewal Center, which promotes traditional training methods and representational art.
