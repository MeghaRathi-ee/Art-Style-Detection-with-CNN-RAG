Post-Impressionism Overview

Post-Impressionism is a predominantly French art movement that developed roughly between 1886 and 1905, from the last Impressionist exhibition to the birth of Fauvism. The term was coined by the British art critic Roger Fry in 1910 for an exhibition of French painting in London. Post-Impressionism is not a single style but rather a collective term for several diverse movements and artists who extended Impressionism while rejecting its limitations. The Post-Impressionists shared a dissatisfaction with Impressionism's emphasis on the fleeting effects of light and color, seeking instead to give art a greater sense of structure, permanence, and emotional depth.

Paul Cezanne (1839-1906) is often called the father of modern art because his innovations laid the groundwork for virtually every major art movement of the 20th century. Cezanne sought to treat nature by the cylinder, the sphere, the cone, reducing natural forms to their geometric essentials. His paintings of Mont Sainte-Victoire, still lifes, and bathers demonstrate his systematic approach to color and form, building up the picture surface through carefully placed patches of color that create both a sense of three-dimensional structure and an awareness of the flat picture plane. His work directly influenced both Cubism and Abstract art.

Vincent van Gogh (1853-1890) transformed Impressionist technique into a vehicle for intense emotional expression. His paintings feature thick, swirling brushstrokes, vibrant and often non-naturalistic colors, and a dynamic sense of movement that conveys his passionate engagement with the world. Works like The Starry Night (1889), Sunflowers (1888), and his self-portraits combine close observation of nature with a highly personal, emotionally charged vision. Van Gogh's approach to color and brushwork was enormously influential on Expressionism, Fauvism, and many subsequent movements.

Paul Gauguin (1848-1903) rejected Western civilization and traveled to Tahiti in search of a more primitive, authentic way of life. His paintings are characterized by bold, flat areas of intense color, simplified forms with strong outlines, and exotic subject matter drawn from Polynesian culture and mythology. Gauguin's Synthetist approach, which emphasized the synthesis of outward appearances with the artist's inner vision and emotion, influenced the development of Symbolism, Expressionism, and various forms of modern primitivism.

Georges Seurat (1859-1891) developed the technique of Pointillism, applying small dots of pure color to the canvas that blend optically when viewed from a distance. His monumental painting A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte represents a scientific, systematic approach to color and composition that contrasts sharply with the spontaneity of Impressionism. Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec captured the nightlife of Montmartre with bold, graphic compositions influenced by Japanese prints, creating vivid depictions of the performers, entertainers, and habitues of Parisian cabarets and dance halls.


Post-Impressionism Key Artists and Techniques

The Post-Impressionists each developed highly individual techniques that extended and transformed the innovations of Impressionism.

Paul Cezanne developed a revolutionary painting technique based on the careful analysis of color relationships. Rather than mixing colors on the palette, he applied small, parallel patches of color (sometimes called constructive brushstrokes) that modeled form through subtle modulations of hue, temperature, and value. A warm ochre patch might sit next to a cool blue-green, their contrast creating a sense of three-dimensional volume. Cezanne worked slowly and methodically, sometimes spending months on a single painting, building up the surface through many sessions of observation and adjustment. His still lifes, such as Still Life with Apples (1893-94), deliberately violate conventional perspective, with tabletops that tilt forward and objects seen from slightly different viewpoints, asserting the painting's identity as a flat surface rather than a window into illusionistic space.

Vincent van Gogh transformed the Impressionist brushstroke into a vehicle for intense emotional expression. His characteristic technique of applying thick, heavily loaded brushstrokes in swirling, rhythmic patterns creates surfaces that pulse with energy and movement. In The Starry Night (1889), the sky becomes a turbulent sea of swirling blue and yellow strokes, with the moon and stars radiating concentric halos of light. Van Gogh's palette shifted dramatically during his career, from the dark earth tones of his early Dutch period to the brilliant yellows, blues, and greens of his Arles and Saint-Remy periods. His sunflower paintings (1888-89) demonstrate his extraordinary sensitivity to the expressive potential of color, using multiple shades of yellow and gold to create compositions of both visual brilliance and emotional intensity.

Paul Gauguin developed a painting technique he called Synthetism, which involved applying flat areas of bold, unmodulated color within strong outlines, creating a style that drew on medieval stained glass, Japanese woodblock prints, and the decorative traditions of non-Western art. His Vision After the Sermon (1888) marks a decisive break from naturalism, depicting Breton women's religious vision in flat areas of intense red and blue separated by a diagonal tree trunk. In Tahiti, Gauguin refined this technique further, creating paintings like Spirit of the Dead Watching (1892) and Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going? (1897) that combine simplified forms and intense, symbolic colors with subjects drawn from Polynesian life and mythology.

Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec developed a distinctive graphic style influenced by Japanese prints, Degas's compositions, and the techniques of lithographic poster design. His paintings and posters of Parisian nightlife, particularly the performers of the Moulin Rouge, are characterized by bold, calligraphic outlines, flat areas of color, and unconventional compositional cropping that captures the energy and artificiality of theatrical performance.

Extended Analysis and Cross-Movement Connections

The Post Impressionism movement must be understood not in isolation but as part of a broader web of artistic developments. Every movement both responds to what came before and sets the stage for what follows. The artists associated with Post Impressionism were deeply aware of art history and deliberately positioned their work in relation to existing traditions, whether by extending them, reacting against them, or synthesizing elements from multiple sources.

The techniques developed by Post Impressionism artists reflected both their aesthetic aims and the materials available to them. Technical innovation in art is never purely formal; it is always connected to what the artist is trying to express or achieve. New techniques emerge when existing methods cannot adequately express new ideas, and the development of new materials and tools often opens up possibilities that artists had not previously imagined.

The reception and interpretation of Post Impressionism has evolved significantly over time. Works that were controversial or misunderstood in their own time are often reinterpreted by later generations, who bring different assumptions and values to their encounter with art. The critical frameworks used to analyze and evaluate art change as intellectual fashions shift, and works that were once dismissed may later be recognized as groundbreaking.

The legacy of Post Impressionism extends beyond the visual arts into literature, music, architecture, and design. Artistic movements rarely exist in isolation from broader cultural developments, and the ideas and innovations of visual artists often parallel or inspire developments in other creative fields. Understanding these cross-disciplinary connections enriches our appreciation of both the art itself and the cultural moment that produced it.

The market for Post Impressionism art has also evolved dramatically. Works that were initially difficult to sell or were purchased for modest sums have in many cases become extraordinarily valuable, reflecting both genuine appreciation and the complex economics of the art market. The relationship between artistic value and market value is one of the most contested questions in the contemporary art world.

The educational significance of Post Impressionism should not be underestimated. The study of art movements provides insight into the broader cultural, political, and philosophical currents of their time. Art history is not merely a catalog of beautiful objects but a record of human creativity, aspiration, and the endless desire to make sense of experience through visual form. The artists associated with Post Impressionism were responding to the specific challenges and opportunities of their historical moment, and their work illuminates both the universal constants of human creativity and the particular circumstances that shaped its expression.
