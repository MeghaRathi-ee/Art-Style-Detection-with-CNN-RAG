Fauvism Overview

Fauvism was the first major avant-garde art movement of the 20th century. It emerged in France around 1904-1908 and is characterized by the use of bold, vivid, non-naturalistic colors and vigorous, spontaneous brushwork. The name Les Fauves (The Wild Beasts) was given to the group by art critic Louis Vauxcelles after seeing their paintings at the 1905 Salon d'Automne in Paris, where the intensity of their color shocked viewers accustomed to more restrained palettes.

The Fauvists were primarily concerned with the expressive potential of color, using it not to describe the natural appearance of things but to convey emotional and aesthetic sensations. They applied pure, vibrant pigments straight from the tube, often using complementary colors side by side for maximum visual impact. Drawing and perspective were simplified or distorted, and shadows were rendered in color rather than gray or black. The overall effect was one of joyous, exuberant vitality.

Henri Matisse was the leader and most important member of the Fauvist group. His painting The Woman with a Hat (1905), exhibited at the Salon d'Automne, was one of the works that provoked the famous Wild Beasts comment. Matisse's Fauvist works, such as The Joy of Life (1905-06) and The Open Window, Collioure (1905), demonstrate his revolutionary approach to color, using flat areas of intense, unmodulated hues to create compositions of extraordinary decorative beauty and emotional resonance. Matisse later said that his goal was an art of balance, purity, and serenity, and his Fauvist period established the foundations for his lifelong exploration of color and form.

Andre Derain worked closely with Matisse during the summer of 1905 at Collioure in southern France, where both artists produced paintings of unprecedented chromatic intensity. Derain's views of London and the Thames, painted in 1906, use brilliant blues, greens, yellows, and reds to transform familiar scenes into vibrant, almost hallucinatory visions. His work during this period combined the color innovations of the Impressionists and Post-Impressionists with a new boldness and directness.

Other important Fauvist painters included Maurice de Vlaminck, whose landscapes feature thick, aggressive brushwork and explosive color; Raoul Dufy, who developed a distinctive style of lively, decorative paintings; Albert Marquet, whose work was more restrained than that of other Fauvists; and Kees van Dongen, known for his sensuous portraits. Georges Braque was briefly associated with Fauvism before turning to Cubism.

Although Fauvism as a cohesive movement was relatively short-lived, lasting only about three years, its impact on modern art was enormous. By demonstrating that color could be completely liberated from its descriptive function and used as an independent expressive element, the Fauvists opened the door for all subsequent experiments in abstract and non-representational color. Their influence can be seen in German Expressionism, Abstract Expressionism, and Color Field painting, among many other movements.


Fauvism Key Artists and Techniques

The Fauvist painters' revolutionary use of color was grounded in the developments of Post-Impressionism, particularly the color theories of the Pointillists and the bold, non-naturalistic palette of Paul Gauguin and Vincent van Gogh. The Fauvists took these innovations further, using color with unprecedented freedom and intensity.

Henri Matisse applied paint in broad, flat areas of intense, saturated color, often using complementary colors side by side for maximum chromatic vibration. In The Joy of Life (Bonheur de vivre, 1905-06), nude figures dance and recline in a landscape rendered in flat areas of brilliant pink, green, yellow, and blue, with sinuous, arabesque-like outlines defining the forms. Matisse's technique combined the directness of Impressionist observation with a new emphasis on decorative harmony and chromatic intensity.

Matisse's The Woman with a Hat (1905) caused a scandal at the Salon d'Automne with its bold patches of green, violet, red, and yellow paint applied to the figure's face and hat. The painting demonstrates the Fauvist principle that color should express the artist's emotional response to the subject rather than describe its actual appearance. Matisse later stated that exactitude is not truth, arguing that emotional truth was more important than visual accuracy.

Andre Derain worked alongside Matisse at Collioure during the summer of 1905, producing some of the most vibrant Fauvist works. His paintings of the port and surrounding landscape use intense, unmixed colors applied in discrete patches and strokes, creating a mosaic-like surface of chromatic energy. Derain's London paintings (1906), particularly Charing Cross Bridge and The Pool of London, transform the gray city into brilliant visions of blue, orange, green, and red.

Maurice de Vlaminck painted with an almost violent physicality, squeezing paint directly from the tube onto the canvas and applying it with aggressive, sweeping brushstrokes. He claimed to want to burn down the Ecole des Beaux-Arts with his cobalt and vermilion, and his landscape paintings radiate a fierce chromatic intensity that matches his personality. His Restaurant de la Machine at Bougival (1905) demonstrates his characteristic technique of combining bold, impastoed brushwork with intensely saturated colors.

Raoul Dufy developed a lighter, more decorative version of Fauvism, using flowing calligraphic lines and transparent washes of brilliant color to depict scenes of leisure, regattas, and seaside life. His technique of applying color independently of drawing, allowing colored areas to spill beyond their outlines, created a sense of spontaneous, joyful freedom. Georges Braque's Fauvist period, particularly his paintings of L'Estaque from 1906, shows him working with the bold, flat colors and simplified forms characteristic of the movement, before his decisive turn toward Cubism in 1908.

The Summer at Collioure and the 1905 Salon

The explosive birth of Fauvism can be traced to the summer of 1905, when Henri Matisse and Andre Derain worked together at the small Mediterranean fishing village of Collioure in southern France. Inspired by the brilliant southern light and liberated from the restraints of Parisian convention, both artists pushed their use of color to unprecedented extremes. They applied pure, vivid pigments straight from the tube, using complementary colors side by side for maximum visual impact, and abandoned the naturalistic description of form in favor of flat, boldly colored shapes defined by strong outlines.

When their paintings were exhibited at the Salon d'Automne in Paris in the autumn of 1905, they caused an immediate sensation. The works were hung together in a single gallery, and their explosive color struck viewers with an almost physical force. Art critic Louis Vauxcelles, seeing a small Renaissance-style bronze sculpture in the center of the room surrounded by the wildly colored canvases, reportedly remarked: Donatello au milieu des fauves, meaning Donatello among the wild beasts. The name stuck, and the artists embraced it.

Matisse's The Woman with a Hat, a portrait of his wife Amelie, was one of the most controversial works in the exhibition. The face is rendered in patches of green, violet, red, and yellow, with bold strokes of color that bear no relation to natural flesh tones. The hat and dress are equally abstracted into areas of intense, non-descriptive color. The painting provoked outrage among conservative critics and viewers, but it also attracted the attention of the American collectors Gertrude and Leo Stein, who purchased it, beginning an important relationship between the Steins and the Parisian avant-garde.

Individual Styles Within Fauvism

Despite the common label, the Fauvist painters had quite different approaches to color and form. Matisse was the most thoughtful and systematic, developing a carefully considered approach to color harmony and decorative composition that he would refine throughout his long career. His Fauvist works, such as The Joy of Life from 1905 to 1906, combine brilliant color with sinuous, arabesque-like outlines that create compositions of extraordinary decorative beauty. Matisse later stated that his goal was an art of balance, purity, and serenity, devoid of troubling or depressing subject matter.

Andre Derain's Fauvist paintings are characterized by a mosaic-like application of small, distinct patches of intense color, creating vibrant, shimmering surfaces. His views of London, painted in 1906, transform the gray city into brilliant visions of blue, orange, green, and red. Maurice de Vlaminck painted with an almost violent physicality, squeezing paint directly from the tube and applying it with aggressive, sweeping brushstrokes. He claimed to want to burn down the Ecole des Beaux-Arts with his cobalt and vermilion.

Raoul Dufy developed a lighter, more decorative version of Fauvism, using flowing calligraphic lines and transparent washes of brilliant color. His technique of applying color independently of drawing, allowing colored areas to spill beyond their outlines, created a sense of spontaneous freedom. Georges Braque's Fauvist period, particularly his paintings of L'Estaque from 1906, shows him working with bold, flat colors before his turn toward Cubism in 1908.

Decline and Lasting Impact

Fauvism as a cohesive movement was remarkably short-lived, lasting only about three years from 1905 to 1908. By 1908, most of the artists had moved on: Braque and Derain turned to Cubism, while Matisse continued to develop his own highly individual approach to color and form. However, the movement's impact was enormous and enduring. By demonstrating that color could be completely liberated from its descriptive function and used as an independent expressive element, the Fauvists opened the door for virtually all subsequent experiments in abstract and non-representational color. Their influence can be traced directly through German Expressionism, Abstract Expressionism, and Color Field painting.
