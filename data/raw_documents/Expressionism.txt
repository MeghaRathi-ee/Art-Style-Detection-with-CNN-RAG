Expressionism Overview

Expressionism was a modernist movement that originated in Northern Europe around the beginning of the 20th century. Its typical trait is to present the world solely from a subjective perspective, distorting it radically for emotional effect in order to evoke moods or ideas. Expressionist artists sought to express the meaning of emotional experience rather than physical reality, using exaggerated colors, distorted forms, and agitated brushwork to convey feelings of anxiety, alienation, and spiritual yearning.

The movement developed in two main groups in Germany: Die Brucke (The Bridge), founded in Dresden in 1905 by Ernst Ludwig Kirchner, Fritz Bleyl, Erich Heckel, and Karl Schmidt-Rottluff; and Der Blaue Reiter (The Blue Rider), formed in Munich in 1911 by Wassily Kandinsky and Franz Marc. Die Brucke artists used jagged, angular forms, bold outlines, and harsh, non-naturalistic colors to create works of raw emotional power. They were influenced by African and Oceanic art, medieval German woodcuts, and the work of Vincent van Gogh and Edvard Munch.

Edvard Munch is often considered a precursor and major influence on Expressionism. His painting The Scream (1893) has become an iconic image of modern anxiety and existential dread. Munch's work explored themes of illness, death, sexuality, and psychological anguish with a directness and emotional intensity that profoundly influenced the German Expressionists. His use of swirling lines, distorted perspectives, and emotionally charged colors established a visual language for expressing inner psychological states.

Ernst Ludwig Kirchner was the leading figure of Die Brucke. His paintings of Berlin street scenes depicted modern urban life with angular, elongated figures, clashing colors, and a sense of nervous energy and alienation. Other important Die Brucke artists included Emil Nolde, whose paintings of religious subjects and landscapes featured intense, almost violent color; Max Pechstein; and Otto Mueller.

The Der Blaue Reiter group was more diverse and intellectually oriented than Die Brucke. Kandinsky, its leading figure, moved progressively toward pure abstraction, believing that color and form could express spiritual truths directly without reference to the material world. Franz Marc painted animals in bold, symbolic colors, viewing them as more spiritually pure than humans. Other members included August Macke, Paul Klee, and Gabriele Munter.

Austrian Expressionism produced two of the most intense and psychologically penetrating painters of the movement: Egon Schiele and Oskar Kokoschka. Schiele's raw, contorted self-portraits and figure studies pushed the boundaries of physical and psychological exposure. Kokoschka's early portraits seemed to penetrate beneath the surface of his subjects to reveal their inner psychological states.

Expressionism had a profound influence on subsequent art movements, including Abstract Expressionism, Neo-Expressionism, and various forms of figurative painting. Its emphasis on subjective emotional experience, the rejection of conventional beauty, and the use of distortion for expressive purposes remain central to contemporary art practice.


Expressionism Key Artists and Techniques

Expressionist painters developed distinctive techniques to convey intense subjective emotion and psychological states, deliberately distorting form, color, and space for expressive effect.

Die Brucke (The Bridge) artists revived the woodcut as a primary artistic medium, embracing its rough, angular qualities as ideally suited to their raw, emotionally direct aesthetic. Ernst Ludwig Kirchner created woodcuts and paintings with jagged, angular forms, harsh outlines, and clashing colors that conveyed the nervous energy and alienation of modern urban life. His Berlin Street Scene paintings (1913-15) depict the city's inhabitants as elongated, mask-like figures in vivid, acid colors, creating a vision of urban modernity as simultaneously exciting and threatening.

Edvard Munch, the great precursor of Expressionism, developed a technique of painting with long, sinuous brushstrokes of thinned paint that created a sense of pulsating, emotional energy. In The Scream (1893), the swirling lines of the sky and landscape echo the agonized figure's silent cry, creating a powerful visual expression of existential anxiety. Munch often reused and reworked themes across different media, creating paintings, prints, and drawings of the same subjects over many years.

Ernst Ludwig Kirchner painted with bold, rapid brushstrokes using vivid, non-naturalistic colors applied directly from the tube. His palette featured acid yellows, electric blues, and hot pinks that intensified the emotional impact of his subjects. The Die Brucke artists often painted together, sharing models and techniques, and deliberately cultivated a raw, unfinished quality that rejected academic polish.

Emil Nolde, who was briefly associated with Die Brucke, developed an intensely personal style characterized by thick, expressive brushwork and saturated, almost violent color. His paintings of religious subjects, such as The Last Supper (1909), transform familiar biblical scenes through the sheer intensity of their emotional expression. Nolde's watercolors of flowers and landscapes achieve extraordinary chromatic brilliance through his bold, wet-on-wet technique.

Egon Schiele's unflinching self-portraits and figure studies feature contorted, angular bodies rendered with aggressive, wiry lines and raw, exposed flesh tones. His technique involved drawing directly with brush and paint, creating images of startling psychological and physical vulnerability. Oskar Kokoschka's early portraits, such as his poster for The Dreaming Youths (1908), use scratchy, nervous lines and muted colors to penetrate beneath his subjects' social facades and reveal their inner psychological turmoil.

Wassily Kandinsky, the leading figure of Der Blaue Reiter, progressively moved from Expressionist landscapes toward pure abstraction, creating paintings in which color and form functioned independently of representational content. His theoretical writings, particularly Concerning the Spiritual in Art (1912), argued that color and form could express spiritual truths directly, without the mediation of recognizable subject matter.

The German Expressionist Groups

Expressionism developed primarily through two organized groups in Germany that had distinct but overlapping aims and methods. Die Brucke, meaning The Bridge, was founded in Dresden in 1905 by four architecture students: Ernst Ludwig Kirchner, Fritz Bleyl, Erich Heckel, and Karl Schmidt-Rottluff. The group chose its name to suggest a bridge to the art of the future. They were influenced by the raw emotional power of African and Oceanic art, the bold color and distorted forms of Van Gogh and Munch, and the expressive woodcuts of medieval German art. The group lived and worked communally, sharing studios and models, and deliberately cultivating a bohemian lifestyle that rejected bourgeois conventions.

Der Blaue Reiter, meaning The Blue Rider, was formed in Munich in 1911 by Wassily Kandinsky and Franz Marc. The group was more diverse and intellectually oriented than Die Brucke, encompassing a wider range of styles from figurative Expressionism to pure abstraction. Kandinsky, the leading figure, progressively moved toward pure abstraction, believing that color and form could express spiritual truths directly without reference to the material world. His theoretical writings, particularly Concerning the Spiritual in Art published in 1912, argued that art was entering a new spiritual epoch in which abstract forms would replace representational imagery.

Franz Marc painted animals in bold, symbolic colors, viewing animals as more spiritually pure and closer to nature than corrupt, materialistic humans. His paintings of blue horses, yellow cows, and red deer use color symbolically rather than naturalistically, with blue representing spirituality and masculinity, yellow representing femininity and joy, and red representing violence and earthly passion.

Austrian Expressionism and Edvard Munch

Austrian Expressionism produced two of the most psychologically intense painters of the 20th century. Egon Schiele created drawings and paintings of the human body that are among the most unflinching depictions of physical and psychological vulnerability in the history of art. His contorted self-portraits and figure studies feature angular, emaciated bodies rendered with aggressive, wiry lines, often in explicit sexual poses that shocked contemporary audiences. Schiele was briefly imprisoned for producing what authorities considered obscene art.

Oskar Kokoschka's early portraits seemed to penetrate beneath the surface of his subjects to reveal their inner psychological states. His technique used scratchy, nervous lines and muted colors to strip away social facades. His expressionist plays and his series of allegorical paintings exploring his turbulent relationship with Alma Mahler demonstrate the movement's extension beyond painting into literature and theater.

Edvard Munch, though Norwegian rather than German, was the single most important precursor and influence on Expressionism. His Frieze of Life series explored themes of love, anxiety, death, and the cycle of human existence with a directness and emotional intensity that profoundly influenced the German Expressionists. The Scream, with its writhing sky and agonized figure, has become the iconic image of modern anxiety.

Techniques and Materials

Expressionist painters deliberately rejected the polished techniques of academic painting in favor of raw, direct methods that emphasized emotional expression over technical refinement. Die Brucke artists revived the woodcut as a primary artistic medium, embracing its rough, angular qualities. They also adopted a technique of rapid, direct painting using bold, unmixed colors applied with thick, visible brushstrokes. Many worked with palette knives as well as brushes, and some applied paint directly from the tube.

The Expressionists' use of color was among their most revolutionary contributions. They used color not to describe the natural appearance of objects but to express emotional and psychological states. A face might be painted green or blue, a sky might be rendered in acid yellow, trees in vivid purple. These non-naturalistic color choices were deliberate and systematic, based on the artists' understanding of color's emotional and symbolic associations.

Legacy and Influence

Expressionism's emphasis on subjective emotional experience, the rejection of conventional beauty, and the use of distortion for expressive purposes remain central to contemporary art practice. The movement directly influenced Abstract Expressionism in America, Neo-Expressionism in the 1980s, and various forms of contemporary figurative painting. Its insistence that art should express inner psychological truth rather than outer physical reality established a principle that continues to shape artistic practice and theory.
