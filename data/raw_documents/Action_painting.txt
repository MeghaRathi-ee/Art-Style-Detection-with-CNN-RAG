Action Painting Overview

Action painting, sometimes called gestural abstraction, is a style of painting in which paint is spontaneously dribbled, splashed, or smeared onto the canvas rather than being carefully applied. The resulting work emphasizes the physical act of painting itself as an essential aspect of the finished work. The style was widespread from the 1940s until the early 1960s and is closely associated with Abstract Expressionism.

The term was coined by the American critic Harold Rosenberg in 1952 in his essay The American Action Painters. According to Rosenberg, the canvas was an arena in which to act. The actions and means for creating the painting were considered of higher importance than the result. This represented a major shift in aesthetic perspective, redefining art as an act rather than an object, a process rather than a product. This redefinition laid the foundation for later movements including Happenings, Fluxus, Conceptual Art, Performance Art, and Installation Art.

Action painting emerged in the context of post-World War II America, a period when quantum mechanics and psychoanalysis were changing people's perception of the physical and psychological world. The artists drew on Carl Jung's studies of archetypal images and Sigmund Freud's theories of the subconscious. The paintings were not meant to portray objects or specific emotions but rather to touch the observer deep in the subconscious mind, evoking a sense of the primeval and tapping into a collective sense of archetypal visual language.

Key action painters included Jackson Pollock, who laid his canvases on the floor and poured, dripped, and flung paint using sticks, trowels, and hardened brushes; Willem de Kooning, known for his aggressive, slashing brushwork and his Women series; Franz Kline, who created dramatic large-scale black-and-white paintings with powerful brushstrokes; and Grace Hartigan, who combined gestural energy with references to popular culture and urban life. Other notable practitioners included Joan Mitchell, Elaine de Kooning, Norman Bluhm, Sam Francis, and Philip Guston.


Action Painting Techniques and Key Works

Action painting techniques varied widely among practitioners, but all shared an emphasis on the physical act of painting as central to the meaning of the artwork. The process of creation was considered as important as, or more important than, the finished product.

Jackson Pollock's technique is the most iconic example of action painting. Working with the canvas unstretched on the floor of his barn studio in Springs, Long Island, Pollock moved around and over the canvas, pouring and dripping commercial house paint (especially Duco enamel) from cans using sticks, hardened brushes, and even his hands. He controlled the flow of paint through the speed and angle of his movements, the height from which he poured, and the viscosity of the paint. The resulting webs of interlacing lines created complex, rhythmic compositions without a traditional focal point. Major works include Number 1A (1948), Lavender Mist (1950), Autumn Rhythm (1950), and Blue Poles (1952).

Willem de Kooning's action painting involved a more traditional relationship with the canvas but was equally physical and intense. He worked with large brushes loaded with thick oil paint, attacking the canvas with slashing, scrubbing, and sweeping motions. His technique involved constantly reworking the surface, applying paint, scraping it off with palette knives, and reapplying. De Kooning's Woman series (1950-53) combined figurative imagery with abstract gestural energy, and his later abstract landscapes achieved a remarkable synthesis of fluid movement and structural clarity.

Franz Kline created monumental black-and-white paintings using house painting brushes on large canvases. His bold, sweeping strokes of black paint against white grounds suggest architectural structures, bridges, and industrial forms, though Kline insisted they were purely abstract. Despite their apparent spontaneity, Kline's compositions were carefully developed from small studies that he projected onto large canvases using a Bell-Opticon projector. Key works include Painting Number 2 (1954) and Mahoning (1956).

Grace Hartigan combined the gestural energy of action painting with references to popular culture, urban life, and art history. Her paintings, such as Grand Street Brides (1954), merge abstract and figurative elements with bold color and vigorous brushwork. Joan Mitchell created intensely colored, multi-panel paintings inspired by landscape and memory, working from her studio in Vetheuil, France, near Monet's former home at Giverny.

Historical Context and Origins

Action painting developed as a response to the political and cultural upheaval following World War II. The movement took place during a period when quantum mechanics and psychoanalysis were beginning to flourish and were changing people's perception of the physical and psychological world. American action painters pondered the nature of art as well as the reasons for the existence of art. The preceding art of Kandinsky and Mondrian had freed itself from the portrayal of objects and instead tried to evoke emotions and feelings within the viewer. Action painting took this a step further, using both Jung and Freud's ideas of the subconscious as its underlying foundations.

Many of the painters were interested in Carl Jung's studies of archetypal images and types, and used their own internal visions to create their paintings. Along with Jung, Sigmund Freud and Surrealism were also influential to the beginning of action painting. The paintings of the action painters were not meant to portray objects per se or even specific emotions. Instead they were meant to touch the observer deep in the subconscious mind, evoking a sense of the primeval and tapping the collective sense of an archetypal visual language. This was done by the artist painting unconsciously and spontaneously, creating a powerful arena of raw emotion and action in the moment.

Over the next two decades after Rosenberg's coining of the term, his redefinition of art as an act rather than an object, as a process rather than a product, was influential and laid the foundation for a number of major art movements, from Happenings and Fluxus to Conceptual Art, Performance Art, Installation Art, and Earth Art.

Legacy and Cross-Movement Connections

Action painting was closely related to French Tachisme, though the two movements developed largely independently. While action painting emphasized the existential drama of the creative act, Tachisme, practiced by artists like Georges Mathieu and Hans Hartung, focused more on the materiality of paint and the spontaneity of the mark.

The influence of action painting extended far beyond the visual arts. The Beat writers, particularly Jack Kerouac, drew parallels between Pollock's spontaneous painting method and their own practice of spontaneous prose. Kerouac's concept of automatic writing was directly inspired by the action painters' emphasis on unmediated creative expression. In music, free jazz musicians like Ornette Coleman and John Coltrane pursued similar ideals of spontaneous improvisation. The Happenings of Allan Kaprow, who had studied with John Cage, extended the concept of action painting into live performance, creating events in which the boundaries between art and life were deliberately blurred.
